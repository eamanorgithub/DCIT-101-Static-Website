The most common technique to prove the unique existence of a certain object is to first prove the existence of the entity with the desired condition, and then to prove that any two such entities (say, {\displaystyle a}a and {\displaystyle b}b) must be equal to each other (i.e. {\displaystyle a=b}a=b). For example, to show that the equation {\displaystyle x+2=5}x+2=5 has exactly one solution, one would first start by establishing that at least one solution exists, namely 3; the proof of this part is simply the verification that the equation below holds: {\displaystyle 3+2=5.}{\displaystyle 3+2=5.} To establish the uniqueness of the solution, one would then proceed by assuming that there are two solutions, namely {\displaystyle a}a and {\displaystyle b}b, satisfying {\displaystyle x+2=5}x+2=5. That is, {\displaystyle a+2=5{\text{ and }}b+2=5.}{\displaystyle a+2=5{\text{ and }}b+2=5.} By transitivity of equality, {\displaystyle a+2=b+2.}{\displaystyle a+2=b+2.} Subtracting 2 from both sides then yields {\displaystyle a=b.}{\displaystyle a=b.} which completes the proof that 3 is the unique solution of {\displaystyle x+2=5}x+2=5. In general, both existence (there exists at least one object) and uniqueness (there exists at most one object) must be proven, in order to conclude that there exists exactly one object satisfying a said condition.
